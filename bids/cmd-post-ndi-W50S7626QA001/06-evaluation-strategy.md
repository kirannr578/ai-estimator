# Evaluation strategy — comparative evaluation (FAR 12.203(c)(2))

This is **NOT LPTA**. It is **NOT a typical FAR Part 15 best-value-tradeoff RFP either**. It is the **FAR Subpart 13.106 simplified acquisition + FAR Part 12 commercial-item procedures + FAR 12.203(c)(2) comparative evaluation** mechanism. The strategy is meaningfully different from the LPTA strategy used for the USFWS sibling bid.

## A. What the solicitation actually says — verbatim from Section M

From `Solicitation - W50S7626QA001 Cmd Post and NDI.pdf` p.49–50, Section M, **52.212-2 Evaluation — Commercial Products and Commercial Services (Feb 2026) (Deviation 2026-O0038) (Tailored)**:

> **(a) Evaluation factors.** The Government will award a contract resulting from this solicitation to the responsible Offeror whose offer conforming to the solicitation will be most advantageous to the Government, price and other factors considered. The following factors will be used to evaluate offers:
>
> **(i)** The submitted quotes, if conforming to the solicitation, (e.g. stated terms, conditions, representations, certifications, timely, and respond to all other information as required), will be reviewed by the Contracting Officer to ensure the Offeror is not debarred, suspended, or proposed for debarment. Offerors found to be debarred, suspended, or proposed for debarment shall be excluded from receiving a contract award and will be removed from the competition.
>
> **(ii)** Quotes meeting the requirements of para (i) will be evaluated in pursuit of the best value for the Government, price and any factors of the solicitation considered. **In accordance with RFO FAR 12.203(c)(2), a comparative evaluation, without objective standards, of the quotes will be conducted.**
>
> **(iii) Past Performance:** Information for past performance reviews may come from either an information system or personal knowledge of an Offeror. Information may be sourced from systems such as, but not limited to, the Contractor Performance Assessment Reporting System (CPARS), the Supplier Performance Risk System (SPRS), or the Contracting Officer's personal knowledge of work performed on any previous federal contract. **If unfavorable information is discovered or known of an Offeror, the Offeror may be removed from the competition and further evaluation.**
>
> **(iv) Technical Review (other factor(s)):** The Government will review the 1) list of equipment/materials/labor disciplines, and 2) project schedule, items described under "Instructions for Submission of Quote," to determine if the Offerors understand the scope of work and demonstrate a thorough knowledge and understanding of the requirement.
> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;**a. Subfactor 1**: The list of equipment, materials, and labor disciplines shall list those items necessary to accomplish the scope of work identified in the statement of work. This list will be used to evaluate the Offeror's understanding of the technical requirement through analysis of the major work elements proposed.
> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;**b. Subfactor 2**: The project schedule will be evaluated relative to overall coordination of the construction phase, to include ability to identify all necessary work elements of the specific project, the Offeror's ability to schedule the activities in a logical sequence, and to ensure contract performance time required in this solicitation is met.
>
> **(v)** The Government intends to award a contract, **without negotiating, to the quote that represents the best value to the Government**; however, the Government reserves the right to negotiate if deemed in its best interest. Examples as to why the Government may deem negotiations to be in its best interest are, but are not limited to: prices/costs are unreasonably low/high, or costs are found to be unbalanced. If the need to negotiate arises, the Government intends to only negotiate with the Offeror being considered for award and represents the anticipated best value to the Government. The Government reserves the right to consider a quote which offers more than the minimum and select that quote if it provides a benefit to the Government. **The Government has the discretion to accept other than the lowest priced quote, if determined to be the best value to the Government.**

## B. Implications

### 1. **Past performance is gateway-AND-discriminator.**
Unlike LPTA where past performance is binary "Acceptable / Not Acceptable", here **the CO can remove an offeror from competition** for unfavorable past performance, AND can use favorable past performance to weight the comparative evaluation. **Strong CPARS history wins.**

- Pull the firm's most recent **2–3 federal CPARS reports** for similar small interior renovation work (especially DoD facility work).
- Pull the firm's **SPRS NIST 800-171 Basic Assessment** score (DFARS 252.204-7019 + 252.204-7024 visibility).
- If the firm has DoD interior-renovation work in the past 3 years, lead the past-performance narrative with the DoD project. If the closest project is non-DoD federal (e.g., DOI, USFWS, USDA), still cite it — federal is federal.
- If the firm is **new to federal work**, this is a meaningful disadvantage in this evaluation framework. Mitigate by (a) leading with state/municipal-government project past-performance (similar oversight + procurement disciplines), (b) emphasizing key personnel resumes with DoD experience, (c) providing reference letters from prior customers.

### 2. **Technical Review is two well-defined deliverables — not a narrative.**
This is the most consequential structural difference vs. a Part 15 RFP:
- **Subfactor 1**: A list of equipment, materials, and labor disciplines — explicitly described as "An example submission would be an RSMeans output list or a similar product." The CO is looking at this as a **completeness check + comprehension check** — does the offeror understand the trade scope?
- **Subfactor 2**: A project schedule — Gantt or task list — showing logical sequencing and 90-day fit.

**This is NOT a place for a 30-page technical narrative.** Resist the temptation to pad. The submission should be:
- **Subfactor 1**: 2–4-page itemized list (CSI / RSMeans format), every trade discipline + material category from `04-scope-of-work.md` enumerated. **Comprehensive but not redundant.**
- **Subfactor 2**: 1-page Gantt covering 90 calendar days, both CLINs in parallel-or-sequence as appropriate, with milestones for: NTP, mobilization, badging coordination, submittal cycle, demo, framing, MEP rough-in, finishes, MEP trim, T&B, fire-protection inspection, pre-final walk at 75% (per SOW §4.8), final at 100% (per SOW §4.9). Show the **Tuesday–Friday 4-day workweek** assumption explicitly.

### 3. **Price is a comparative factor, not a pass/fail.**
The CO can pick something **other than** the lowest price. So:
- **Don't race to the bottom**. A firm with strong CPARS, comprehensive technical submission, and a **clearly-defended price** will win over a firm with weaker past performance and a lowball.
- **Don't be unbalanced** — Section M (a)(v) explicitly says "costs are found to be unbalanced" can trigger discussions. **Don't load CLIN 0001 to subsidize CLIN 0002 (or vice versa).** Each CLIN price = its own scope's direct cost + fair markup share.
- **Don't be unreasonably low** — Section M (a)(v) explicitly says "prices/costs are unreasonably low" can trigger discussions. The CO may have an Independent Government Estimate (IGE) — going > 30–40% below it raises a red flag.
- **Show your work in pricing summary** (do not include the cost breakdown in the submission unless asked, but be ready to defend in negotiations).

### 4. **No formal discussion phase is guaranteed.**
Section M (a)(v): *"The Government intends to award a contract, **without negotiating**…"* — this means **the offer submitted on 04 JUN must be self-defending**. There is no expected opportunity to clarify, supplement, or revise. **One shot.**

### 5. **The Government CAN accept "more than the minimum" if it provides benefit.**
Section M (a)(v) says the Government can take a quote that offers more than the minimum. This means **a quote that demonstrates additional value** (e.g., shorter schedule, more comprehensive QC plan, value-engineering observations, key-personnel CVs) **may win even at a higher price**. This is a meaningful incentive to over-deliver on the technical submission.

## C. Recommended price envelope strategy

Given comparative evaluation (not LPTA) + an unknown number of competitors (likely 3–8 small bidders for an ANG interior renovation in Tarrant County, where there are many DoD-experienced subs and primes), the **competitive sweet spot** is:

| Strategy | Price posture | Win probability | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lowball (5–10% below IGE) | Low margin | **Lower than expected** in comparative evaluation if past-perf is weak | High execution risk; CO may flag unreasonably low and negotiate |
| Market (within ±5% of IGE) | Standard markup ($475K mid-target ± 10%) | **Best balance** when past-performance is solid | Standard |
| Premium (5–15% above IGE) | Loaded markup with documented value-add | Wins only if past-perf is best-in-class AND technical submission demonstrably out-classes competitors | CO may go with more competitive price |

**Recommended**: Target the **market** price envelope at ~$475K combined ($340K CLIN 0001 + $135K CLIN 0002 — see `05-bid-schedule-mapping.md` § D for the floor/mid/ceiling math), and **invest the comparative-evaluation discriminator in the technical submission and past-performance volume**.

> Final pricing waits on:
> 1. Site visit (or exception → field-verify-after-award) — locks the SF assumption
> 2. Sub quotes for secure doors/hardware, electrical, mechanical/HVAC
> 3. Sheet 2 of 2 visual review — locks CLIN 0002 scope
> 4. AFFF residue handling clarification — locks CLIN 0002 environmental scope

## D. What "winning" looks like — pre-mortem

Imagine the CO with 5 quotes on her desk Friday 5 JUN. She goes:

1. **Quick screen** — no one is debarred. All 5 advance.
2. **Pricing scan** — 4 quotes are within $50K of each other, 1 is 30% below (suspicious). She sets the 1 outlier aside for unreasonably-low review.
3. **Past performance** — she pulls CPARS for the 4. Two have strong DoD/federal renovation history. Two have municipal-only history.
4. **Technical** — she opens each Subfactor 1 list. One firm wrote 1 page ("General construction; electrical; HVAC; flooring; misc."). One firm wrote 4 pages with 35 line items including "Stanley/Best Lock-Core compatible hardware coordination", "anti-access bars on supply-air return per Drawing General Note 11", "PFAS-conscious AFFF-room demolition coordination with 136 Env Office", "AF 3000 submittal ESOH review cycle".
5. **Schedule** — same. One firm submitted a 90-day bar chart with 4 bars. Another submitted a milestone-detailed Gantt with 30 tasks, badging coordination dates, submittal-approval cycle baked in, T&B + AHJ inspection sequence.

The fourth firm — strong past perf, comprehensive trade list, detailed schedule — wins, **even if their price is $20K higher than the third firm**.

**Lesson**: invest the differentiating effort in the technical submission. Price discipline matters; technical comprehensiveness wins comparative evaluation.
